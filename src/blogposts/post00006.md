In my free time, I've gotten to start playing through the original Pokemon games, and in playing it, there's one thing that's really stood out to me during this playthrough.  The original games, set in the Kanto region, have one of the most compact, intelligent designs, to the point where I find every corner of the game memorable.  Sure, this could be attributed to the fact that this is the first game, that it is going to be the smallest for that reason, but at the same time, I think it's brilliant how the map slowly opens up over time, how you see different types of road blocks from trees that need to be cut down, to the guards surrounding Saffron City, to a couple of Snorlaxes sleeping at key points of contention, and as you slowly figure out how to get past each of them, the map opens up and forms these complete loops.

The fact that the map is set up in this neat grid-like structure makes getting around quite easy, even without using Fly.  The games do usually find interesting ways to set up roadblocks similar to what was mentioned in future installments, but I think the map in Red and Blue heavily encourages searching for solutions to the roadblock "puzzles".  It makes one consider level design; make your worlds feel big when in reality they are set up to guide you in a specific path, then once you open up all routes, you see how interconnected the world is.  This impacts more than just the layout and worldbuilding, it aids in storytelling, as you get to see how the different NPCs and towns interact with one another as well as the natural environment (forests, mountains, the sea, etc.).
